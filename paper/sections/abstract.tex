\begin{abstract}
Users routinely embed quality thresholds in prompts (``this only needs to be a 70 out of 100''), following official guidance to define explicit success criteria. We test whether these thresholds constrain revision behavior in a two-turn writing setup with 3,840 trials crossing follow-up prompt phrasing (2 levels), stated quality threshold (8 levels), and model (GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet 4, Gemini 2.5 Flash) across eight scenarios. Quality thresholds have \textit{no detectable effect} on whether the model revises. A revision-implying follow-up (``Can this be improved?'') triggers near-universal revision (99.9\%) regardless of threshold, while an evaluative follow-up produces 0.3--38.0\% revision rates depending on model. Within triggered revisions, thresholds produce only a weak-to-moderate dose-response (Spearman $\rho = -0.14$ to $-0.50$). These results are consistent with a \textbf{two-stage account}: Stage~1 (revision gate) is controlled by prompt phrasing; Stage~2 (revision intensity) is modulated by the stated threshold. Under the conversational conditions we tested, models possess threshold-calibration ability but do not apply it at the revision gate.
\end{abstract}
