\begin{abstract}
Official prompting guidance emphasizes defining explicit success criteria, and users follow this advice, routinely embedding quality thresholds in prompts (``this only needs to be a 70 out of 100'') and assuming this constrains model revision behavior. We test this assumption in a two-turn writing-revision setup with 3,840 trials crossing follow-up prompt phrasing (2 levels), stated quality threshold (8 levels), and model (GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet 4, Gemini 2.5 Flash) across eight scenarios. We find that quality thresholds have \textit{no detectable effect} on whether the model revises at all. A revision-implying follow-up (``Can this be improved?'') triggers near-universal revision (99.9\%) regardless of threshold, while an evaluative follow-up produces 0.3--38.0\% revision rates depending on model. Within triggered revisions, thresholds produce only a weak-to-moderate dose-response on overcorrection (Spearman $\rho = -0.14$ to $-0.50$). These results are consistent with a \textbf{two-stage account}: Stage~1 (revision gate) is controlled by prompt phrasing; Stage~2 (revision intensity) is modulated by the stated threshold. The two stages respond to different input signals, and a significant probe $\times$ threshold interaction is consistent with a floor effect rather than true coupling. Under the conversational conditions we tested, models possess threshold-calibration ability but do not apply it at the revision gate, suggesting that user-stated thresholds may be largely ineffective under revision-implying follow-ups.
\end{abstract}
