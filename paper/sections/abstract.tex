\begin{abstract}
Users routinely embed quality thresholds in prompts (``this only needs to be a 70 out of 100''), assuming this constrains model revision behavior. We test this assumption in a two-turn writing-revision setup with 3,840 trials crossing follow-up prompt phrasing (2 levels), stated quality threshold (8 levels), and model (GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet 4, Gemini 2.5 Flash) across eight scenarios. We find that quality thresholds have \textit{no detectable effect} on the upstream decision of whether the model revises at all. A revision-implying follow-up (``Can this be improved?'') triggers near-universal revision (99.9\%) regardless of threshold, while an evaluative follow-up produces 0.3--38.0\% revision rates depending on model. Within triggered revisions, thresholds produce only a weak-to-moderate dose-response on overcorrection (Spearman $\rho = -0.14$ to $-0.50$). We characterize this as a \textbf{two-stage account}: Stage~1 (revision gate) is controlled by prompt phrasing; Stage~2 (revision intensity) is modulated by the stated threshold. The stages are functionally separable: a significant probe $\times$ threshold interaction reflects a floor effect (the evaluative probe suppresses revision, leaving no overcorrection to modulate) rather than true mechanistic coupling. Whether this threshold-blind compliance pattern reflects sycophancy or straightforward instruction-following, the practical consequence is the same: models possess threshold-calibration ability but gate it behind a phrasing-sensitive decision that shows no detectable sensitivity to the user's stated quality bar, suggesting that user-stated thresholds may be largely ineffective under revision-implying conversational flows.
\end{abstract}
