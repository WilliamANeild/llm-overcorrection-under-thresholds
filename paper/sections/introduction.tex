\section{Introduction}

Prompting guides from major providers encourage users to define explicit success criteria. OpenAI's documentation recommends defining ``the output contract'' with exact deliverables \cite{openai2025prompting}; Anthropic's guide emphasizes being ``clear and direct'' about desired output \cite{anthropic2025prompting}. Users routinely embed quality thresholds in their prompts (``this only needs to be a 70 out of 100''), treating them as binding constraints \cite{white2023prompt, schulhoff2024prompt}.

But does the model respect these constraints when asked to revise? A user requests a quick text canceling brunch, specifying ``around a 70 out of 100.'' The model drafts two sentences. The user asks, ``Can this be improved?'' and receives a three-paragraph revision overshooting the stated threshold. We call this \textit{overcorrection}, and we observe it across all eight scenarios in our experiment.

We test this in a two-turn writing-revision setup. In a fully factorial experiment with 3,840 trials, we cross follow-up prompt phrasing (2 levels), stated quality threshold (8 levels, with numeric and qualitative framings), and model (Claude Sonnet 4, Gemini 2.5 Flash, GPT-4o) across eight scenarios spanning four register categories. An LLM judge evaluates each revision on a revision gate classification and four rubric dimension scores, with inter-rater reliability verified against a second judge model.

The follow-up prompt's phrasing, not the quality threshold, is the dominant predictor of whether the model revises. A five-probe pilot reveals this starkly: revision rates drop from 100\% to below 25\% based on probe phrasing alone, with no smooth gradient between (Figure~\ref{fig:probe-cliff}). Thresholds do modulate \textit{how much} the model overcorrects once revision is triggered, but this calibration is locked behind a phrasing-sensitive gate. We characterize this as a \textbf{two-stage account}: Stage~1 is a phrasing-sensitive \textit{revision gate}; Stage~2 is a threshold-sensitive \textit{intensity calibration}.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A two-stage account (gate $+$ intensity) of LLM revision behavior, supported by 3,840 trials showing that the decision to revise and the calibration of that revision respond to different input signals, with a significant but floor-effect-driven interaction.
    \item A five-probe calibration study documenting a steep \textit{compliance cliff:} revision rates shift from near-100\% to near-0\% based on probe phrasing, with no observed gradient between (though 5 probes cannot rule out a steep continuous function).
    \item A cross-model comparison showing that revision disposition under evaluative prompts varies sharply across model families (0.3\% to 38.0\% revision rate), while behavior under revision-implying prompts is uniformly compliant.
\end{enumerate}
