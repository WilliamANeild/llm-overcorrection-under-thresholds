\section{Introduction}

OpenAI's prompting documentation recommends defining ``the output contract'' with exact deliverables and expected outcomes \cite{openai2025prompting}; Anthropic's best-practices guide emphasizes being ``clear and direct'' about desired output and specifying constraints up front \cite{anthropic2025prompting}. Users follow this advice, routinely embedding quality thresholds in their prompts (``this only needs to be a 70 out of 100'') and treating them as binding constraints on model behavior \cite{white2023prompt, schulhoff2024prompt}.

But does the model actually respect these constraints when asked to revise? Consider an illustrative case: a user asks for a quick text message canceling brunch plans, specifying the quality only needs to be ``around a 70 out of 100.'' The model produces a reasonable two-sentence text. The user then asks, ``Can this be improved?'' Instead of a minor polish, the model returns a three-paragraph message with a greeting, an apology, and a rain-check proposal. The stated threshold was 70; the revision overshoots it dramatically. This pattern, where models revise far beyond the user's stated quality bar, is what we call \textit{overcorrection}, and we observe it across all eight scenarios in our experiment.

We test this empirically in a two-turn writing-revision setup. In a fully factorial experiment with 3,840 trials, we cross follow-up prompt phrasing (2 levels), stated quality threshold (8 levels, with numeric and qualitative framings), and model (GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet 4, Gemini 2.5 Flash) across eight writing scenarios spanning four register categories. An LLM judge evaluates each revision on a revision gate classification and four rubric dimension scores, with inter-rater reliability verified against a second judge model.

We find that the follow-up prompt's phrasing, not the quality threshold, is the dominant predictor of whether the model revises. A five-probe pilot study reveals this starkly: revision rates drop from 100\% to below 25\% based on probe phrasing alone, with no smooth gradient between (Figure~\ref{fig:probe-cliff}). Thresholds do modulate \textit{how much} the model overcorrects once revision is triggered, but this calibration ability is locked behind a phrasing-sensitive gate that shows no detectable sensitivity to thresholds (Kruskal-Wallis $p > 0.40$ for all models). We characterize this as a \textbf{two-stage account}: Stage~1 is a phrasing-sensitive \textit{revision gate}; Stage~2 is a threshold-sensitive \textit{intensity calibration}. The calibration ability users would most benefit from (having the model decline unnecessary revision) is not activated under the revision-implying conversational conditions we tested.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A two-stage account (gate $+$ intensity) of LLM revision behavior, supported by 3,840 trials showing that the decision to revise and the calibration of that revision respond to different input signals, with a significant but floor-effect-driven interaction.
    \item A five-probe calibration study documenting a steep \textit{compliance cliff}: revision rates shift from near-100\% to near-0\% based on probe phrasing, with no observed gradient between (though 5 probes cannot rule out a steep continuous function).
    \item A cross-model comparison showing that revision disposition under evaluative prompts varies sharply across model families (0.3\% to 38.0\% revision rate), while behavior under revision-implying prompts is uniformly compliant.
\end{enumerate}
