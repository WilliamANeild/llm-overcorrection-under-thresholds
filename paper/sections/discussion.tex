\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{The Two-Stage Account.}
\label{sec:discussion-two-stage}
The results presented above are consistent with a two-stage account of LLM revision behavior in which the decision to revise and the calibration of that revision respond to different input signals. Stage~1 (the revision gate) responds to the pragmatic force of the follow-up prompt with near-binary sensitivity: any phrasing that implies revision triggers compliance; any phrasing that implies evaluation suppresses it. Stage~2 (revision intensity) responds to the user's stated quality threshold with a weak-to-moderate dose-response. The probe $\times$ threshold interaction term is statistically significant ($\beta = -2.20$, $z = -5.69$, $p < 0.001$), but this is consistent with a floor effect rather than true coupling: because the evaluative probe suppresses revision almost entirely, there is no overcorrection variance for the threshold to modulate. Within each probe condition taken separately, the two stages appear to operate on separate inputs: the threshold has no detectable effect on the gate (Kruskal-Wallis $p > 0.40$), and probe phrasing does not modulate calibration intensity within the leading-probe condition.

The calibration ability demonstrated by Stage~2, which shows models \textit{do} modulate revision intensity based on thresholds, is effectively inaccessible at Stage~1 under the conversational conditions we tested. Users who state quality preferences and then ask casual follow-up questions receive revisions that are insensitive to their stated standards.

\paragraph{Threshold-Blind Compliance.}
A natural objection is that ``Can this be improved?'' has a pragmatically obvious answer: almost anything \textit{can} be improved. Whether the gate reflects sycophancy \cite{perez2023discovering, sharma2024towards} or straightforward instruction-following, the key observation is the same: it is threshold-blind. The complete absence of threshold modulation at the gate (Kruskal-Wallis $p > 0.40$) shows that models are not incorporating stated preferences into the revision decision. We cannot distinguish sycophancy from threshold-insensitive instruction-following on behavioral data alone.

\paragraph{The Compliance Cliff.}
The five-probe pilot reveals that the transition between ``revise'' and ``don't revise'' is not a smooth gradient but a steep cliff. Only one probe fell in the intermediate range (25\%), suggesting that the cliff's edge is narrow, though five probes cannot definitively distinguish a binary cliff from a steep continuous function. Whatever the underlying mechanism, small changes in phrasing produce large changes in revision behavior, and users are unlikely to be aware of this sensitivity.

\paragraph{Model Differences.}
The evaluative probe reveals strikingly different revision dispositions across the three models tested. Gemini 2.5 Flash declines revision in 99.7\% of evaluative-probe trials, while Claude Sonnet 4 and GPT-4o show more moderate decline rates (62.0\% and 68.6\%, respectively). Gemini shows the strongest overcorrection tendency when it does revise ($\beta = 1.15$ in Model~C) but is also the model most likely to correctly decline. All three models show comparable threshold sensitivity within the leading-probe condition, suggesting that the divergence is specific to the gate rather than to calibration ability.

\paragraph{Framing Effects.}
Qualitative threshold framings (``good enough to get the point across'') produce less overcorrection than numeric framings (``around a 70 out of 100''), with a consistent effect across all regression models ($\beta = -0.74$ to $-1.05$, all $p < 0.001$). One interpretation is that qualitative framings provide quality descriptions that are more grounded in the model's training distribution, while numeric scores may be harder for the model to map onto revision behavior. Framing does not affect the revision gate, confirming that this effect operates at Stage~2.

\paragraph{Recency and Positional Effects.}
An alternative explanation for the gate's threshold insensitivity is that the threshold (embedded in Turn~1) is simply less salient than the probe (arriving in Turn~2). Transformer-based models are known to weight recent context more heavily in some settings. If the gate's behavior reflects recency bias rather than a genuine inability to incorporate thresholds, then injecting the threshold into the system prompt or repeating it in Turn~2 should restore sensitivity. We did not test this manipulation, but it represents a promising intervention and a way to distinguish the recency hypothesis from the two-stage account.

\paragraph{Design Implications.}
If the two-stage pattern holds beyond our tested conditions, it suggests that interfaces may need to protect user-stated quality preferences from conversational pragmatics. Possible directions (all untested) include injecting thresholds into the system prompt, rewriting follow-up prompts to reference stated thresholds, or adding a pre-revision gate classifier.
